How to Choose the Right Tees in Golf

A simple rule many coaches use: take your average driver carry distance and multiply it by roughly 36. That gives you a ballpark total yardage for a course that will feel fair but still challenging.

Playing tees that are too long turns every hole into a grind. You’re hitting long irons or hybrids into every green, pace of play slows down, and scores balloon. Playing a tee that’s slightly shorter usually means more wedges, more birdie chances, and more fun.

Golf Tee Distance Guide by Driver Carry

Use this quick tee distance guide as a starting point. Then refine it with the calculator above and your own on-course results.

Average driver carry Suggested total yardage Typical tee box
180–200 yards 4,500–5,200 yards Forward / Red or shorter Gold tees
200–220 yards 5,000–5,700 yards Forward or Senior / Gold tees
220–240 yards 5,700–6,300 yards White / Regular tees
240–260 yards 6,200–6,700 yards White or Blue tees
260+ yards 6,700–7,200+ yards Blue or Back / Championship tees

These numbers are guidelines, not strict rules. Wind, elevation, firmness of the fairways, and how often you hit greens in regulation all influence which tees will feel right for you on a given day.

Why Playing the Right Tees Matters

Most golfers secretly know when they are playing a course that is too long. Every par 4 feels like driver plus long iron, par 3s are all hybrids, and par 5s are three-shot holes even after a solid drive. Choosing the correct tee box can instantly make golf more fun without changing your swing.

Matching your tees to your true driver distance helps:

  • Keep more wedges and short irons in your hands into greens.
  • Lower your scores without any swing change.
  • Speed up pace of play for your group and the course.
  • Reduce frustration from constantly hitting long approach shots.

Simple Rules for Picking Tees

If you are not sure where to start, use these simple rules of thumb:

  • If you rarely reach a par 4 in two shots, move up a set of tees.
  • If you are often hitting wedges into every green, you can experiment with moving back.
  • High-handicap and beginner golfers should almost always start from the forward tees.
  • Seniors, juniors, and players returning from injury should prioritize enjoyment and pace of play over total yardage.

Frequently Asked Questions About Which Tees to Play

  • How do I know which tees I should play from?

    Start with your average driver carry distance. Multiply that number by about 36 to get a comfortable total yardage, then compare that to the scorecard at your course. Our tee selection calculator does that math for you and adjusts for age, handicap, and how tough you want the course to feel.

  • What tees should I play if I drive the ball 200 yards?

    If your typical carry is around 200 yards, look for tees under about 5,000 to 5,400 yards. That usually means forward or shorter senior tees. From there, adjust based on how often you’re reaching par 4s in two and par 5s in three.

  • What tees should I play if I drive the ball 240 yards?

    At 240 yards of driver carry, many golfers score best from roughly 6,000 to 6,400 yards total. On most scorecards that lines up with white or regular tees, or a combo of white and blue if the course offers it.

  • Should my tee choice be based on age, handicap, or distance?

    Distance is the most important input, but age and handicap tell you how consistent you are. Two golfers with the same distance might choose different tees if one is a new player and the other is a low handicap who wants a tougher test. The calculator blends all three to give a balanced suggestion.

  • Is it bad to play a tee box that is too long?

    It’s not “wrong,” but it usually isn’t fun. Playing tees that are too long means more long irons and hybrids into greens, higher scores, and slower pace of play. Most golfers shoot better scores and enjoy the game more when they move up a set of tees.

  • Should beginners always use the forward tees?

    Yes, almost always. New golfers benefit from shorter approach shots, less pressure, and more chances to hit greens in regulation. As confidence, distance, and consistency improve, they can slowly experiment with moving back.

  • Can I use this tee selector for any course?

    Yes. The tool gives you a recommended total yardage and tee color, which you then match to whatever tees are closest on the scorecard at your local course. It works for public, private, resort, and tournament setups.

  • How often should I change which tees I play?

    Any time your distance, fitness, or handicap changes noticeably, it’s worth checking your tee selection again. You can also temporarily move up on very windy, cold, or wet days to keep the course playing fair for your current game.
